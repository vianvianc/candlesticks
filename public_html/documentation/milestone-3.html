In feudal Japan, rice was collected as tax in the different regions. Gradually rice markets sprang up to trade tax-rice with rice bills. The Dojima Rice Exchange in 1697 was the world's first futures market with rice essentially being a currency, a necessary payment to the Tokugawa Shogunate. Trading was held on the roads around the exchange and after the markets closed, water was thrown to disperse lingering crowds. The price of rice would be telegraphed with flag signals to reach the capital.

A wealthy merchant named Homma Munehisa around 1750 began the futures market concept, by mass-distributing rice bale promissory notes. Homma drew bar charts on time intervals (previously unheard of), came up with trading strategies and identified reversal patterns. He is the father of candlestick charting. Homma saw that market sentiment (fear and greed) was reflected in the price and could be visualized in patterns. Here is a painting of Homma on his blanket with his concubines.

In 1989, researcher and trader, Steve Nison published his best-selling book "Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques". This book completely revolutionized the markets. He introduced Hommas 'price action' which is a psychological study of sentiment. Up until then, traders based reversals and continuations on moving averages and other statistical equations. Now, nowhere in the world is a chart represented without candlesticks and all traders, big and small, have some understanding of candlesticks.

Traders who have studied candlesticks patterns have likely become overwhelmed by the sheer number of indicators. Here is a non-comprehensive list of just the reversal patterns. It's too much data to process, especially if they want to trade high-volume or be in positions for minutes.
